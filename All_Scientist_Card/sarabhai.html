Vikram Sarabhai
The Father of the Indian Space Program

A visionary Who Pioneered India's Journey Into Space & Atomic Energy.

Biography & Early Life

Born on August 12, 1919, in Ahmedabad, Gujarat, India, Vikram Ambalal Sarabhai came from an influential family of industrialists deeply involved in India's independence movement. His father, Ambalal Sarabhai, was a prominent industrialist, and his mother, Sarla Devi, was a community leader.

Sarabhai received his early education at his family's private school, "Retreat," which fostered an experimental approach to learning. He later studied at Gujarat College, Ahmedabad, and then moved to England, where he matriculated at St John's College, Cambridge, in 1937.

He pursued his Tripos in Natural Sciences at Cambridge. With the outbreak of World War II, he returned to India and joined the Indian Institute of Science, Bangalore, where he conducted research in cosmic rays under the guidance of Nobel laureate Sir C.V. Raman. He earned his PhD from Cambridge in 1947 for his thesis "Cosmic Ray Investigations in Tropical Latitudes."

Vikram Sarabhai died on December 30, 1971, at the age of 52, in Thiruvananthapuram, Kerala, leaving behind a monumental legacy in Indian science and technology.

Pioneering Scientific Contributions & Institutions

Sarabhai's vision extended across various scientific and institutional fields, making him a true nation-builder:

  • Father of the Indian Space Program: He is universally acknowledged as the "Father of the Indian Space Program." He convinced the Indian government of the importance of a space program for national development, leading to the establishment of the Indian National Committee for Space Research (INCOSPAR) in 1962, which later became the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) in 1969.
  • Physical Research Laboratory (PRL): In 1947, he founded the Physical Research Laboratory (PRL) in Ahmedabad, a pioneering institution for cosmic rays and atmospheric sciences research.
  • Indian Institute of Management, Ahmedabad (IIMA): Recognizing the need for professional management education, he played a pivotal role in establishing the prestigious Indian Institute of Management, Ahmedabad (IIMA) in 1961.
  • ATOMIC ENERGY COMMISSION: Following Homi J. Bhabha's demise, Sarabhai took over as the Chairman of the Atomic Energy Commission of India in 1966, further strengthening India's nuclear research capabilities.
  • Thumba Equatorial Rocket Launching Station (TERLS): He was instrumental in setting up TERLS in Thiruvananthapuram, Kerala, a crucial site for launching sounding rockets for atmospheric studies due to its proximity to the geomagnetic equator. The first rocket was launched in 1963.

His vision was not just about technological prowess but about applying science for the benefit of society, particularly in education, communication, and resource management.

Sarabhai was awarded the Padma Bhushan in 1966 and the Padma Vibhushan (posthumously) in 1972, India's third and second-highest civilian honors, respectively.

Lesser-Known Facts

  • His family's legacy: The Sarabhai family was deeply involved in the Indian independence movement and social reforms. His sister, Mridula Sarabhai, was a prominent freedom fighter. He came from a family of freedom fighters and social reformers.
  • Pioneer in business management: Beyond science, he recognized the importance of professional management. The establishment of IIMA reflects his belief in integrating scientific and managerial excellence. He was a key figure in India's management education.
  • He married a renowned dancer: Vikram Sarabhai was married to the celebrated classical dancer Mrinalini Sarabhai, and their daughter Mallika Sarabhai is also a renowned dancer and activist. He was married to a famous classical dancer.
  • Role in SITE program: He initiated the Satellite Instructional Television Experiment (SITE) in 1975, which used satellite technology to bring educational programs to rural India. This was a unique experiment in global education through space. He launched educational TV through satellites.
  • A polymath's vision: Sarabhai's interests spanned a wide range, from cosmic rays and space technology to textiles, pharmaceuticals, and management. He believed in applying scientific principles to various aspects of national development. He applied science across diverse fields.
  • His memorial: The Vikram Sarabhai Space Centre (VSSC) in Thiruvananthapuram is ISRO's largest facility and is named in his honor, commemorating his contributions to space research. India's leading space research center is named after him.

Impactful Thoughts & Quotes By Vikram Sarabhai

“There are some who question the relevance of space activities in a developing nation. To us, there is no ambiguity of purpose. We are convinced that if we are to play a meaningful role nationally, and in the comity of nations, we must be second to none in the application of advanced technologies to the real problems of man and society.”

Main point: Space for societal development.
Sarabhai's conviction that space technology is vital for solving real-world problems in developing nations.

“A nation which can develop its own satellite and launching capability would have the means to make its own future.”

Main point: Self-reliance in space technology.
Emphasizing that indigenous space capabilities are crucial for a nation's sovereignty and progress.

“I have always believed that for science and technology to flourish in a country, there must be a basic core of dedicated individuals, committed to excellence.”

Main point: Importance of dedicated individuals in science.
Highlighting the role of passionate and excellent researchers in scientific advancement.

“Education is the most powerful weapon which you can use to change the world.”

Main point: Education as a transformative tool.
Though often attributed to Mandela, this sentiment resonates strongly with Sarabhai's efforts in establishing educational institutions.

“The development of a nation depends on its ability to apply science and technology to the problems of its people.”

Main point: Science for national development.
His core philosophy that scientific progress should directly serve the needs and challenges of the populace.

“The future of India lies in the integrated development of science and technology for the betterment of society.”

Main point: Integrated scientific development for societal good.
A holistic view emphasizing the synergy between scientific and technological advancements and societal welfare.
